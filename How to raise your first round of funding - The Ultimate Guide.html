How to raise your first round of funding - The Ultimate Guide

So, it seems you want to raise capital. For most people, this decision isn’t taken with a lot of thought behind it -- you see others raising money and you feel an urgent need to do the same or you feel like you’re left behind. I’ve raised money three times in my life, for two separate companies. The first two times were well thought out, the most recent time was me “going with the flow” rather than a well thought out funding round -- it also happened to be the most amount of capital we have raised.

I’m writing this note to myself and anyone else who might be preparing to raise, and will be going through all the nitty gritties of completing a successful fund raise. A lot of what I will describe here is “insider information”, in the sense that only people privy to the way the indian VC/startup ecosystem works will know this. But I am your unfair advantage, so it is my duty to break the veil open for you.

Firstly, what are the different types of rounds? These “stages” of rounds reflect where the company is in its journey. Here is the rough breakdown:

Pre-Seed: 100-500k, Idea stage

Seed: 500k-3 Million, Initial product stage

Pre-Series A: 1 Million-3 Million (there is an overlap between Seed and Pre-Series A, you can call it whatever as long as it is in this range); early signal of Product Market Fit, some evidence that the market loves and uses your product, at least a segment of the market

Series A: 3 Million+, Strong signal of Product Market Fit - the market loves and uses the product, it fills a need

Series B: Usually 10 Million+, Proven PMF, Scaling like crazy

Series C, D, E: Variable, Mature startup with a huge user/customer base

The types of investors in these different rounds also varies: pre-seed investors are typically angels, seed investors are either institutional funds (who also raise money from other investors) or angels, and Pre-Series A, Series A and beyond are typically institutional investors. While angels can write checks and don’t really care much because it is their own money, institutional investors raise external capital from high net worth individuals, angels, and other funds (they call these external investors Limited Partners, or LPs for short). The problem with them raising from LPs is that they have to write lengthy reports, do due diligence (proper background check on the company), and justify their decisions to their LPs later, so it is naturally harder to raise from them.

Since most people reading this will be raising a pre-seed or seed round first, this newsletter will cover that stage of fundraising. We’ll ignore the later rounds for now. I’m going to break down the fundraising process into 2 broad steps: Outline, and Depth.

Stage 1: Outline

At the surface level (first meeting), most investors have 4 primary questions about the business.

  1. Story
  2. Traction
  3. Team
  4. Market

In an investor’s head, you need to check off these 4 boxes to proceed to the next level.


Story

Generally at the Seed stages, you need to have a strong story, or a “why” of your business. Why did you get into this? Why did you see a need to build this business? How is it special to you versus, say, running a restaurant? And money of course, is not a good answer to this question. Most individuals at this stage invest in passionate entrepreneurs building good products who really care about what they are doing.

Traction

The next obvious question is whether you have any users or customers for your product. This stage is tricky in the sense that if you have built something valuable before, investors are willing to completely overlook traction for a new project. If you are new to the ecosystem, investors are more likely to want to see good traction. Remember, this is the first meeting, so they aren’t really going to dive into details -- they just want to see surface level data and whether what you have built has some value to your user base.

Team


A good team signals two things: One, that the founders can convince other solid people to join them on their mission, and two, that the product can actually be built and reach where it has to. Remember that there is usually a gap between the story that is told and the product that exists. The story is one of an ideal world where the product gets big, but the reality is that the product is probably at version 0.1. It will take years before this happens and you need a good team to pull it off. What will mostly be measured is if the team has the domain experience to solve this: It would be rare to see a construction engineer solve a climate change problem or a climate change expert solve a skincare problem. Although, there is one major exception - Computer Science Engineers In the last ten years, this one category of engineers have proven they can take on any problem in any domain and win. It is less because they are skilled, but more because computer science is a force multiplier in any domain. Even if they know less about the particular domain, adding computer technology and internet access to it allows it to scale to millions of users, so the potential revenue is always higher. For example, you can make the best jeans but it's a much better business to make an online store where all the jean makers can sell their jeans at different prices and to a larger audience. The headache of producing each item is with the vendors but your one time scalability has the potential to reach a household at the other end of your nation; that's how powerful CS and the internet is.

One important clarification here; when I use the words “Computer Science Engineer”, I don’t mean those who have passed out of college and hold a degree. I mean anyone who can code. People who can engineer a solution using Computer Science, it's that simple. My definition of something does not have anything to do with a degree.  


Market

The final thing that investors want to see is whether the market is big enough. Let’s take our own example -- when we built Scenes and realized that it works, the next question is “How many people would it work for?”. The answer is roughly 100 million English speaking people in India. We know this because LinkedIn has 80 million English speakers in India, and another 10-20 million kids who are still in an English medium school or college. That is the ceiling of what we can reach.

Similarly, if you start an Uber for Dogs (a service to allow people to book cars to transport dogs), even if it works at a small scale, nobody will invest in it. The main reason is that the market for such a service is tiny! The number of people in India who would book a cab purely for their dog is very, very small. Investors want to bet big -- the venture funding game is an all or nothing game. If you want to build a small business, bootstrap is the way to go.

Let’s assume the investor likes your pitch so far and wants to dive deeper. The next step is more grueling and takes longer -- usually 2-5 months.

Stage 2: Diving Deeper

This step takes place over several meetings (over several weeks to months). The smaller the round, the faster it closes. The larger the quantum, the more time it takes. The more investors in the round, the more time it takes. The less known you are, the more time it takes.

To give you context: our last round, which had 27 investors, took 2 months to close. This is despite the fact that we already knew most of the investors beforehand.

Investors at this stage will want to have these questions answered:

  1. Are the founders full time? If not, chances of raising are not that high. Investors want to see commitment to a project and personal risk taken. Who would give you money if you are already employed and doing this on the side?

  2. Is this venture scalable? Is this the next Facebook (with a billion user market), or an Uber for Dogs, with a market of 20,000 people? Investors, especially institutional investors, have a goal: they invest in companies that have a potential of returning their entire fund size. For example, if the VC firm itself raised 200 million dollars in their round, they need to invest in companies that can sell for at least 200 million dollars. The reason for this is that 9/10 VC bets fail. So there needs to be at least one company among their bets that can get really really big and make up for the losses on the others. Small bets are automatically shut off.

  3. Is there a moat? A moat is a question of defensibility. Remember Tiktok? When TikTok came out and did well, there were thousands of clones. What advantage did Tiktok have over the others apart from their brand? Is it a technological defensive point (like AI) or a community defensive point (like Avalon’s Army)? Either way, companies that get big without a moat have a risk of dying to generic clones.

  4. Do we have a pre-existing relationship with this company? Avalon is not fundraising right now, but that doesn’t stop us from continually meeting new VCs and chronically giving them updates. What you absolutely don’t want is to have a great product but have no pre-existing relationships with funds or angels. It’s hard to build a relationship and trust with someone in a few days. Typically you need at least a few people in the round you know well, and hope that they give you warm introductions to other investors.
    The good thing is that when you are building something nice and active on Twitter (not Instagram or Tiktok), VC analysts will reach out to you. Analysts have a job role that involves them scouting for interesting companies and keeping tabs on their progress. It’s a good idea to build relationships with these analysts and keep them in the loop (think of it as content marketing).

  5. Is this company safe to invest in? VCs run a process called due diligence in parallel with their talks with you. In this process, they check your incorporation and legal status, and also talk with a few people you have worked with in the past (which is why it is important to keep good relationships with your old co-workers). Finally, they will talk to a few of your users to see if their feedback matches up with the story you have told the VC. What will be valuable to create at this point is a Due Diligence document, which I will cover in a bit.

  6. Who else is investing? I’ve made a parody video on this a long time ago, but this is how it really works. It is hard to make an investment decision in a company, you will usually not know much about how it works or how it is run. A good signal that it might be a good investment is if other well known investors jump in the loop. The more such investors who are subscribing to your round, the more new investors who jump on in the bid to not be left out. Of the three times I’ve raised capital, the third one was a “hot round”, where we were oversubscribed (too many investors, too few slots), and had to leave some investors out and disappointed.
    This is why I say that the most critical thing to have is a few good investors who commit first, and the round momentum builds up as you get more such people involved.

  7. Is there social proof that this works? Looking at other investors is one thing, but is there buzz from users about this startup? Are people on Twitter talking about it? (the reason I mention Twitter is because most analysts are on twitter).

  8. Traction Finally, what does the data say? There are a few metrics that VCs want to see, depending on the type of startup you are. I will be making a very long post in the coming weeks about these metrics (including our own and what are good benchmarks); but will also be running you through them briefly in a bit.

The best way to tackle these questions is to “pre-answer” them for the VCs. That means that you create the right documentation and decks for them to read whenever they want, instead of interrogating you. That way, you only have to pitch the outline (Stage 1), and your documents allow them to go deeper later without asking you.

The Documents

Here are the documents that Avalon puts together to pre-answer those questions.

1. A pitch deck

This is an overview of the outline that we spoke about before, with some deeper answers on metrics embedded inside. A pitch deck is a visual representation of your story and charts -- don’t bore people with too much text or going too far in depth. These will usually be circulated over email and nobody has the patience to read through 50 slides, especially when cold contacted. VCs will also circulate this deck to all their LPs. The best pitch decks to model around are the FrontApp deck and the Mixpanel Deck. While they’re not seed stage decks, the structure is the same -- and they’re very well done! This deck should be sent before your meeting.

2. A Product Vision document

This is where you should go in depth, and what you should send to investors after your first meeting. Feel free to go in depth explaining what you are building, why it is different, and where it lies compared to the competition.

3. A Data document

This is where you describe all the traction you have seen so far, including your downloads, sign ups, activated users, retention, daily and monthly active users, net promoter score, k-factor, churn, attribution, and revenue. We’ll go over all of these metrics and more in a later post. These metrics might change depending on what your business is.

4. Cap table

The cap table is a list of who has invested and what percentage of the company each investor and founder owns. At the seed stage, the ideal amount of dilution to have is 17-22%, and 10% of the company is reserved for employee stock options (also called ESOP pool). So founders should end up with 65-80% of the company after the round. VCs don’t typically invest in solo founders, so make sure you have co-founders you can count on.

Let’s assume a fund/angel has taken interest in your company and loves your documents and vision. The next step is to actually get the round closed. With angels, this is easy -- if they verbally say “yes”, then you are done for now. With a VC, it is a little more tricky.

You see, VCs actually have a hierarchy of power inside the fund. Analysts (sometimes just titled “Investors” on LinkedIn)  are the guys/girls who scout for companies and go through your documents. If they like your company, they typically arrange a meeting for you and a Partner. A Partner is the person who will lead you round and will also sit on your board (I’ll make a post about board dynamics later). If a partner likes you, he will arrange a meeting with you and an investment committee (also called an IC). The IC is basically a group of all the influential people in a fund, who vote on whether to invest in a business or not.

Sidenote: At the seed stage, most people in the IC don’t all agree via consensus on whether to invest or not -- there is simply not enough data. Therefore for sub 1 Million dollar rounds, most Partners in the top 5 VC firms in India can take solo decisions. They only need IC approval for bigger check sizes.

If an IC has said yes to you, or a partner has made a unilateral decision to invest, then the next stage is a termsheet. A termsheet runs through all the basic terms of the deal. With termsheets, the shorter the better. At this point, you get all the funds and angels to sign off on this, and the final process is to encode the short termsheet into a long Shareholder’s agreement. All of this will be done by your lawyers and fairly standard, so no stress here.

And...that’s it! The money will be wired into your bank account, and at this point you are a funded company. Happy operating :)
